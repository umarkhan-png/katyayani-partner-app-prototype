Internal Product Doc v1.0 · Apr 2026

We're building for
the dukaan wala.
Not the consumer.

Every screen, every label, every tap in the Katyayani Partner App is designed around one question: does this help a semi-literate agri retailer buy faster, earn more, and trust us more? If not, it doesn't ship.

01 · The Problem We're Solving
🐌
Onboarding is slow and painful
The old app asked for everything at once — forms, documents, GST, Aadhaar — in one long flow. Drop-off was high because users didn't know how many steps were left or why each was needed. And even after completing it, verification took days with no status updates, leaving new partners on hold before they could place even one order. We rebuilt this with a step-by-step flow and instant verification so partners know exactly where they stand at every point.
📦
Homepage showed a category grid, not what to buy
The old home screen was a flat category list — Pesticides, Fertilisers, Seeds. It put the entire navigation burden on the retailer. They had to know what they wanted, find the right category, scroll 80 products, then decide. No intelligence, no seasonal context, no "you usually buy this." The homepage worked like a warehouse shelf, not a buying assistant.
💸
Margin was hidden — the one number retailers care about
Retailers don't buy for themselves — they buy to resell. Their entire decision is based on margin. The old app showed only MRP and selling price, tucked inside a product detail page. There was no bulk-tier pricing visible, no scheme benefit calculation, no "what do I actually earn per bottle?" Retailers had to do mental math on every product, every time. And a PDP that doesn't answer "will this sell and what do I earn?" is a PDP that doesn't convert.
📉
App looked basic — far behind competitor benchmarks
Partners compared the app to Agrim, AgroStar, and Nurture.farm — apps that have invested heavily in design, features, and trust. The old version had no visual personality — flat white screens, generic layout, no image-forward product cards, no trust signals. No schemes surfaced, no reorder logic, no AI tools, no community features. It felt like a form attached to a catalog. In a market where retailers have real options, "functional" is not enough — the app has to feel purposeful, modern, and built specifically for agri trade.
Our answer
Rebuild every one of these four failure points — step-by-step onboarding with instant verification, a homepage that surfaces what the retailer will buy before they search, margin on every card and every tier, and a visual standard that matches the apps retailers already compare us against.
02 · Who We're Building For
Our partners are not in metros. They are in Tier 3 towns and villages.
Katyayani's distribution reach goes deep into rural India — towns like Sheopur (MP), Parbhani (Maharashtra), Sikar (Rajasthan), Navsari (Gujarat). These are not young, tech-savvy buyers browsing on high-speed Wi-Fi. They are shop owners who learned UPI from their son, whose first smartphone is still running Android 9, and who judge an app by how quickly they can get back to attending a customer at the counter.
Primary User — The Tier 3 Agri Retailer
🏪
Who they are
Owns a small agri inputs shop — 200 to 600 sq ft — in a Tier 3 town or large village. Sells pesticides, fertilisers, seeds, and crop nutrition products. Serves 15–50 farmers daily during peak season. Monthly purchase value: ₹1–6L depending on season.
📍
Where they are
Tier 3 towns and rural market towns (qasbas). Poor or inconsistent 4G. Devices are budget Android phones (₹6,000–12,000 range). The shop may not have a computer. Business records are in a physical register.
📱
Their digital life
WhatsApp is their operating system. They use it for orders, payments, price lists, and customer queries. YouTube for entertainment. UPI (PhonePe/Google Pay) for payments. They have not used an e-commerce app designed for B2B before — Katyayani Partner App may be their first.
🧠
How they decide what to buy
What's selling right now in the village. What farmers are coming in and asking for. What gave the best margin last season. Word of mouth from neighbouring shopkeepers. They don't research — they restock based on what moved off the shelf.
⚠️
What they fear
Being cheated on price or quality. Getting fake or substandard product. Delivery taking too long when season is on. Apps that waste their time with steps that don't make sense. Anything that requires reading a lot of English text to understand.
What This Means for UI
✓
Design for slow internet. No heavy animations, no large uncompressed images, no functionality that breaks on 2G. Every screen must be usable on a low-end device in poor network conditions.
✓
Hindi + short English. Labels in both scripts. Language that a Class 8 pass person can read without effort. No jargon, no long sentences.
✓
Large tap targets. Minimum 44px touch areas. A retailer is often tapping quickly between serving customers — mis-taps are frustrating and erode trust.
✓
One action per screen. No screen should require more than one decision. A user who is confused does not tap — they close the app.
✓
Margin always visible. This is a business app. Show % margin on every product card, every price tier, every order line. The retailer should never need to calculate.
✓
Trust signals everywhere. Verified badge, price match guarantee, real delivery dates, Katyayani branding. A Tier 3 retailer has been cheated before. Earn their trust visually before asking for their money.
03 · UI Philosophy — 6 Core Principles
01
User-Friendly & Intuitive Onboarding
Guided onboarding with instant document verification
Onboarding is the hardest step. The retailer doesn't know us yet. Each screen guides them with clear instructions, visual examples, and a step indicator so they always know how far they are. Documents are verified instantly using Document ID APIs and AI — no waiting days for a human review. The partner gets access fast and starts ordering with confidence.
One screen, one job Guided with examples Instant AI verification
02
Decision-First Homepage
Show what they'll buy before they look
A retailer opens the app with a job to do — restock. We don't show them a category grid that requires navigation. We show curated sections: seasonal must-haves, last month's reorders, schemes expiring soon. The product they need is in the first scroll.
Section-wise, not category-wise Context before catalog
03
Margin Transparency
Profit is never hidden
The retailer is running a business. Their most important number is margin. We show it on the product card, in the price tier table, on the cart summary, and on the order confirmation. Green always means "you earn this." Never decorated, never buried.
Margin % always in green Bulk discount tiers visible
04
Trust Before Conversion
PDP earns the order before checkout asks for it
The Product Detail Page is where purchase decisions are made — not the cart. So the PDP must answer every question a retailer has before they tap "Add": What does it do? Which crop? What margin do I earn? What do others buy? What schemes apply? We load the PDP with all of this — scheme details, bulk tiers, seasonal suitability, verified product info, price match guarantee — so by the time they reach checkout, the decision is already made. No pressure, no shortcuts.
PDP = decision engine No dark patterns No hidden charges
05
Keep It Simple
Simple screens. Clear actions. No confusion.
A new retailer should never feel overwhelmed. Every screen has one primary action. Product cards show name, price, margin — and nothing else. Complexity is kept out of the main flow. A user who is confused does not tap — they close the app.
One primary action per screen Less is more
06
Structured Feature Rollout
Features in phases. Core always first.
The app has many features — but they are never mixed together on one screen. Each feature lives in its own clearly separated section so the experience never feels overloaded. Features go live in phases: only after the current phase hits its success metric do we unlock the next one. Metrics decide what stays, what improves, and what gets removed — not assumptions.
Separate sections, no clutter Phase-gated releases Metrics decide keep or remove
04 · Key Design Decisions Explained
Onboarding Why we made onboarding step-by-step
❌ What we avoided
Long registration form: Name, shop name, GST, PAN, address, Aadhaar, license — all on one screen.
No progress indicator. No explanation of why each field is needed.
Upload all documents at once before getting any access.
VS
✓ What we built
One screen = one document. License first (most familiar to the retailer), then Aadhaar, then shop photo.
Video guidance on each screen — show them exactly what the document should look like.
Progress bar always visible. "Step 2 of 4" — the end is in sight.
Explain why each doc is needed: "To verify you're a licensed pesticide dealer."
Design insight
The biggest drop in any B2B onboarding is the document upload step. We modeled it after Rapido's driver onboarding — India's most successful gig-economy onboarding — where each document gets its own screen, its own explanation, and its own camera UI. Result: completion rate goes up because the user never feels lost mid-flow.
Homepage Sections over categories
❌ Category-first grid
Home shows: Pesticides / Fertilisers / Seeds / Fungicides / Micro-nutrients.
Retailer must know what category they want, tap in, browse 80 products, find what they need.
Zero intelligence — same experience for new user and 2-year veteran.
VS
✓ Intent-driven sections
🔥 Trending in your area — what other retailers in the same region are ordering this week.
🌱 Season must-haves — based on current crop calendar (Kharif/Rabi/Zaid).
🎁 Active schemes — buy 5 get 1, cashback offers expiring soon — shown before regular products.
🔁 Reorder — products from last 2 orders surfaced immediately.
📦 Bestsellers — consistent top-movers for newer retailers.
Design insight
A retailer's monthly stock is ~80% repeat. If we surface their reorder list before they even search, the session time drops from 5 minutes to 45 seconds. The homepage isn't a catalog — it's a restock assistant that knows your shop.
Pricing Margin in green, Off% in gray — always
The rule
Margin % — always in emerald green (#0E7A4E). This is the retailer's income. Green = money in your pocket.
Off% / Discount — always in gray (#9E9E9E). This is a relative number vs MRP — useful context, not the main message.
These two numbers are never the same color. A retailer should be able to glance at a card and instantly know: green number = what I earn.
Why not red for discount?
Consumer apps use red for discounts to create excitement. This app is B2B. Red = danger, out-of-stock, warning. We never use red for positive signals.
If everything is red and bold, nothing is an alert. We reserve red for: low stock warnings, verification failure, payment errors.
Anti-pattern No "10-minute delivery" / lightning badges
This is B2B agri. Delivery is next-day or scheduled — typically 24–72 hours. Consumer quick-commerce conventions (⚡ Express, 🟢 10 min, countdown timers) would be lies. We use EDD (Estimated Delivery Date) instead — a specific date, not a vague "express" badge. Honest delivery communication builds more trust than exciting-but-false claims. One bad delivery experience undoes 10 good ones.

The one metric above all

07 · What We Do Not Do
🚫
No lifestyle photography
Farmers in fields, happy families, green meadows. This is an inventory purchasing app. Product-on-white only.
🚫
No "10-min delivery" badges
B2B agri delivery is next-day/scheduled. False urgency destroys trust permanently.
🚫
No colorful filled icons
Outline/stroke SVGs only. Colorful cartoon icons feel like a children's app — unprofessional for B2B.
🚫
No shipping all features at once
Features go live only when the previous phase's success gate is hit. Complexity before trust = churn.
🚫
No redesigning from scratch
Always build on what exists. Add, refine, improve — never nuke a tested flow to replace it with something "cleaner".
🚫
No red for discounts
Red = alert/danger in this app. Off% stays gray. Margin stays green. Color carries meaning — don't dilute it.
